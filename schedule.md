## Weeks 1–2: Short, easy intro ￼

Focus on getting used to the brace without irritation.

- Wear it 1 time per day, 20–30 minutes.

- Aim for 4–5 days per week.

- Use it during something low‑intensity and mostly stationary: computer work, reading, or light chores at home.

- When you take it off, try to keep the same “tall, open” posture for 5–10 minutes without it.

If it feels like too much, stay at 20 minutes for the whole first two weeks.

## Weeks 3–4: Build to a solid daily block ￼

Now you can give your body a clearer “this is your ideal posture” window each day.

- Wear it 1 time per day, 45–60 minutes.

- Aim for 5 days per week.

- Keep it to a consistent window (for example: first hour of focused work, or early evening at home).

- After you remove it, keep checking in with your posture over the next hour and gently correct yourself when you notice slouching.

## Weeks 5–6: Same length, less dependence ￼

Here you’re maintaining the same block but relying more on your muscles.

- Still 1 session per day, 45–60 minutes, but only 3–4 days per week.

- Choose your “worst posture” time (afternoon slump, long meeting block, etc.).

- Pair it with a tiny strength routine right after you take it off (5–10 minutes is enough):

 ▫ Something like rows (band or dumbbells), light back-of-shoulder work, plus a plank or dead bug.

- Think of the brace now as a form teacher, not support gear.

## Weeks 7–8: Phase out to occasional use ￼

Now you mainly use the brace as a reminder on tougher days.

- 1 session per day, 20–30 minutes, 2–3 days per week.

- Use it only when you’re extra tired, have a long sit, or feel especially slouchy.

- Keep up the posture checks and light strength work even on days you don’t wear it.

## Daily “micro‑checks” (brace or no brace) ￼

A few times per day, quickly scan for:

- Feet stable, not wrapped around chair legs.

- Hips not tucked under; sit on your sit bones.

- Ribcage stacked over pelvis, not flared forward.

- Shoulders gently down and back, not yanked.

- Head over shoulders, chin slightly tucked (not jutting forward).
